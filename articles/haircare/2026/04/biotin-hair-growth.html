Biotin — vitamin B7, also known as vitamin H — is the best-selling hair supplement in the United States, anchoring a category worth over $2 billion annually. The supplement is marketed with structure/function claims linking biotin to "healthy hair growth," "stronger nails," and "radiant skin." These claims are permitted by the FDA under DSHEA without requiring evidence of efficacy — only a disclaimer that the statement has not been evaluated by the FDA.

The biological rationale for biotin in hair health is real but narrow: biotin is an essential cofactor for carboxylase enzymes involved in amino acid metabolism, and severe biotin deficiency causes alopecia among other symptoms. The critical question is whether supplementation above adequate intake levels provides any additional benefit for hair growth in individuals who are not biotin-deficient — which is the vast majority of the supplement-buying population.

The Claim

"Biotin supports healthy hair growth" — a structure/function claim implying that biotin supplementation will improve hair growth, thickness, or reduce hair loss in the general population.

Biotin Biochemistry

Biotin serves as a covalently bound cofactor for five carboxylase enzymes in humans: acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACC1 and ACC2), pyruvate carboxylase, propionyl-CoA carboxylase, and 3-methylcrotonyl-CoA carboxylase. These enzymes are involved in fatty acid synthesis, gluconeogenesis, and amino acid catabolism. The connection to hair specifically involves keratin synthesis: biotin-dependent carboxylases contribute to the amino acid pool used for keratin production in hair follicle matrix cells.

In biotin deficiency, impaired carboxylase activity disrupts these metabolic pathways, and alopecia is a well-documented clinical manifestation alongside dermatitis, conjunctivitis, and neurological symptoms. Supplementation in deficient individuals reliably reverses these symptoms. The extrapolation from "deficiency causes hair loss" to "supplementation above adequacy improves hair growth" is the logical gap that the marketing exploits.

The Deficiency Question

Biotin deficiency is rare in the general population. The adequate intake (AI) for adults is 30 mcg/day, and typical Western diets provide 35–70 mcg/day from foods including eggs, liver, nuts, and legumes. Additionally, intestinal bacteria synthesize biotin, contributing to total body stores. True biotin deficiency occurs primarily in specific populations: individuals with biotinidase deficiency (a genetic disorder), chronic anticonvulsant use, prolonged parenteral nutrition without biotin supplementation, chronic alcoholism, or excessive raw egg white consumption (avidin binds biotin).

Hair supplement doses typically range from 2,500 to 10,000 mcg — 83 to 333 times the adequate intake. There is no established mechanism by which supraphysiological biotin intake would enhance keratin synthesis in individuals with adequate biotin status. The water-soluble nature of biotin means excess is excreted renally, not stored — the body cannot "bank" extra biotin for enhanced hair production.

What the Evidence Actually Shows

There are no published randomized controlled trials demonstrating that biotin supplementation alone improves hair growth in biotin-replete individuals. The studies cited in biotin marketing either used multi-ingredient supplements (making biotin's contribution unquantifiable), did not assess baseline biotin status, or studied biotin-deficient populations. The absence of evidence is not proof of absence — but for a $2B+ category, the lack of even a single well-designed RCT of biotin monotherapy in replete subjects is notable.

Lab Interference

A clinically significant concern with high-dose biotin supplementation is interference with immunoassay-based laboratory tests. Biotin at supplement doses (2,500–10,000 mcg) can cause falsely elevated or falsely decreased results in assays that use streptavidin-biotin technology — which includes many common tests for troponin (cardiac marker), thyroid function (TSH, free T4), and hormone levels. The FDA issued a safety communication in 2017 warning of this interference after at least one death was attributed to a falsely low troponin result in a patient taking high-dose biotin.

This is not a theoretical concern. A 2019 study found that 7.4% of samples submitted to a reference laboratory had biotin concentrations high enough to interfere with at least one immunoassay. Patients taking biotin supplements should be advised to discontinue for 72 hours before laboratory testing — advice that is rarely included in supplement marketing or labeling.

Verdict: Claim Unsupported

The claim that biotin supplementation improves hair growth in the general population is not supported by clinical evidence. No RCT has demonstrated benefit of biotin monotherapy in biotin-replete individuals. Biotin deficiency is rare, and supplementation at typical hair-supplement doses (83–333x the adequate intake) has no established mechanism of benefit in replete individuals. The lab interference risk from high-dose biotin is a documented safety concern that is inadequately communicated. Evidence rating: 1/5.
