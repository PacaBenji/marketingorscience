Androgenetic alopecia (AGA) affects approximately 50% of men by age 50 and 40% of women by age 70. Despite sharing the same diagnostic label, male and female pattern hair loss differ in presentation, hormonal drivers, and — critically — in the strength and breadth of treatment evidence available. The marketing of hair loss products frequently treats AGA as a single condition with universal solutions. The clinical reality is more nuanced.

Male AGA follows a well-characterized pattern of bitemporal recession and vertex thinning (Hamilton-Norwood classification), driven primarily by DHT-mediated follicular miniaturization. Female AGA typically presents as diffuse thinning over the crown with preservation of the frontal hairline (Ludwig classification), with a more complex hormonal picture involving both androgens and estrogen decline.

This review examines the RCT evidence for the major hair loss treatments — minoxidil, finasteride, spironolactone, and PRP — stratified by sex, to clarify where the evidence supports gender-neutral marketing and where it does not.

Hormonal Divergence

In men, AGA is driven primarily by DHT binding to androgen receptors in genetically susceptible follicles. The 5-alpha reductase enzyme converts testosterone to DHT in the dermal papilla, and the miniaturization process is relatively straightforward: block DHT, slow or reverse miniaturization. This is why finasteride (a 5-AR inhibitor) works well in men — it addresses the primary driver.

In women, the hormonal picture is more complex. While androgens play a role, female AGA involves additional factors: declining estrogen (particularly post-menopause), relative androgen excess, and potentially different androgen receptor sensitivity patterns. The diffuse thinning pattern suggests a different follicular response to hormonal signals compared to the focal miniaturization seen in men. This biological divergence has direct implications for treatment selection and expected response.

What the Evidence Actually Shows

Minoxidil is the only treatment with strong RCT evidence in both sexes, though the FDA-approved concentration differs (5% for men, 2% for women — though 5% foam has since been approved for women). Finasteride has robust male evidence but limited and mixed female evidence, with the additional barrier of teratogenicity risk. Spironolactone is used almost exclusively in women but lacks the large RCT evidence base that finasteride has in men. PRP has similar (moderate) evidence quality in both sexes.

The net result: men have two well-evidenced first-line options (minoxidil + finasteride), while women have one strong option (minoxidil) and several moderate-evidence alternatives. Gender-neutral marketing obscures this asymmetry.

The Finasteride Gap

Finasteride's male-only FDA approval creates the most significant evidence gap between the sexes. In men, finasteride 1 mg produces hair count increases of approximately 10–15% and prevents further loss in ~85% of users. In post-menopausal women (where teratogenicity is not a concern), the evidence is mixed: some trials show modest benefit at higher doses (2.5–5 mg), while others show no significant difference from placebo. The largest female finasteride RCT (Yeon et al., 2011, n=87) showed improvement in only 47% of women at 1 mg versus 53% at 5 mg — response rates substantially lower than in men.

This gap is not simply a matter of insufficient research. The biological rationale for finasteride is weaker in women: female AGA is less consistently DHT-driven, and the diffuse thinning pattern may reflect mechanisms that 5-AR inhibition does not address. The marketing of "DHT-blocking" products to women without acknowledging this evidence gap is misleading.

Verdict: Partially Supported

Gender-neutral hair loss marketing is partially supported for minoxidil (which has evidence in both sexes) but not supported for DHT-blocking treatments, where the male evidence is substantially stronger. The treatment landscape for female AGA is narrower and less well-evidenced than for male AGA. Products marketed identically to both sexes without acknowledging this divergence overstate the evidence for the female population. Evidence rating: 3/5.
