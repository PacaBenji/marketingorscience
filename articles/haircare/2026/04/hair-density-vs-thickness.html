"Clinically proven to increase hair growth." This claim appears on products ranging from minoxidil to biotin supplements to laser combs. But "hair growth" is not a single measurement — it encompasses at least three distinct endpoints: hair density (number of hairs per unit area), hair diameter (thickness of individual shafts), and total hair mass (the product of count and diameter). These measurements capture different aspects of hair loss and respond differently to treatment.

The distinction matters because androgenetic alopecia is fundamentally a disease of miniaturization — follicles produce progressively thinner hairs before eventually ceasing production entirely. A treatment that increases hair count by 10% but does not reverse miniaturization produces a different clinical outcome than one that increases hair diameter by 10% in existing follicles. Marketing that conflates these endpoints obscures what a treatment actually does.

Defining Endpoints

Hair density refers to the number of terminal and vellus hairs per unit area, typically measured in a 1 cm² target area on the scalp. This is the most common primary endpoint in hair loss RCTs and is measured by manual counting from macrophotographs or by automated analysis (phototrichogram, TrichoScan). An increase in hair count indicates either new hair growth from previously dormant follicles or the transition of vellus hairs to countable size.

Hair diameter (shaft thickness) is measured in micrometers and reflects the degree of follicular miniaturization. In AGA, terminal hairs (thick, pigmented, >60 µm) progressively miniaturize to vellus hairs (thin, unpigmented, <30 µm). A treatment that reverses miniaturization — increasing the proportion of terminal hairs — may not change total hair count but can substantially improve perceived volume and coverage.

Total hair mass (or cumulative hair thickness) is the product of hair count and average diameter. This metric best correlates with perceived hair volume but is rarely reported as a primary endpoint. A treatment could increase hair count while average diameter decreases (more hairs, but thinner) — producing a positive "hair growth" result that does not translate to visible improvement.

Measurement Methods

Phototrichogram is the standard method for hair density measurement in clinical trials. A small area of scalp is clipped, photographed at baseline and follow-up, and hairs are counted manually or by software. The method is reproducible but measures only density in the target area — it does not capture diameter changes or global hair volume.

TrichoScan is a digital epiluminescence system that measures both hair density and hair diameter simultaneously, providing data on terminal/vellus ratio and cumulative hair thickness. It is more informative than phototrichogram alone but is used in fewer trials. Global photography with investigator assessment captures overall cosmetic change but is subjective and less reproducible.

What the Evidence Actually Shows

The majority of hair loss RCTs report only hair count as the primary endpoint. Hair diameter and total hair mass are rarely reported, despite being more clinically relevant for AGA (a miniaturization disease). This means that "clinically proven hair growth" claims based on these trials refer specifically to hair count increases — which may or may not translate to visible improvement in hair volume, depending on the diameter of the new or retained hairs.

The Miniaturization Problem

In AGA, the clinically meaningful change is the reversal of miniaturization — converting vellus hairs back to terminal hairs. A treatment that increases hair count by adding vellus-caliber hairs produces a statistically significant result on the primary endpoint but minimal cosmetic benefit. Conversely, a treatment that increases the diameter of existing miniaturized hairs without changing total count could produce substantial cosmetic improvement while showing no change on the standard primary endpoint.

Finasteride is notable for producing both count increases and diameter increases (reversal of miniaturization), which is why its cosmetic outcomes tend to exceed what hair count data alone would predict. Minoxidil primarily increases hair count and prolongs anagen, with less consistent evidence for diameter reversal. This mechanistic difference is clinically relevant but is not captured by the standard "hair growth" marketing claim.

Verdict: Mixed Evidence

The claim of "clinically proven hair growth" is technically accurate for treatments with positive hair count data, but misleading when it conflates density and diameter endpoints. Most trials measure only hair count, which does not fully capture the miniaturization reversal that determines cosmetic outcome in AGA. Consumers and clinicians should ask specifically what endpoint was measured and whether diameter data is available. Evidence rating: 3/5.
