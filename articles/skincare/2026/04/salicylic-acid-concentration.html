Salicylic acid is the dominant beta-hydroxy acid (BHA) in the OTC skincare market, appearing in cleansers, toners, serums, and spot treatments at concentrations ranging from 0.5% to 2% (the FDA OTC monograph maximum for leave-on products). Concentration is a primary marketing differentiator: "maximum strength 2%" commands premium pricing over 0.5% formulations, with the implicit claim that higher concentration delivers proportionally better results.

The pharmacology of salicylic acid is more nuanced than concentration alone suggests. SA is a lipophilic acid that penetrates the pilosebaceous unit to exfoliate within the pore — but its efficacy depends critically on formulation pH, the proportion of un-ionized (active) acid, vehicle characteristics, and contact time. Two products at the same labeled concentration can deliver substantially different amounts of active SA to the target site.

The Claim

"Maximum strength 2% salicylic acid" — implying that higher concentration delivers superior acne-clearing or exfoliation outcomes compared to lower concentrations.

(Composite representative claim from OTC salicylic acid product marketing.)

Mechanism

Salicylic acid is a lipophilic beta-hydroxy acid with a pKa of approximately 2.97. Its mechanism of action in acne involves desquamation of the follicular epithelium (comedolytic activity), reduction of sebaceous gland activity at higher concentrations, and mild anti-inflammatory effects. SA penetrates the lipid-rich environment of the pilosebaceous unit more effectively than water-soluble alpha-hydroxy acids, which is the basis for its preferential use in acne versus AHAs.

The lipophilic penetration is concentration-dependent but not linearly so. At typical OTC concentrations (0.5–2%), the rate-limiting factor for efficacy is often not the total SA concentration but the proportion that exists in the un-ionized (protonated) form — which is determined by the formulation pH relative to SA's pKa.

The pH Factor

The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation governs the proportion of un-ionized salicylic acid at a given pH. At pH 2.97 (equal to the pKa), 50% of SA is un-ionized. At pH 3.5, approximately 25% is un-ionized. At pH 4.0, only about 10% is un-ionized. Since only the un-ionized form penetrates the stratum corneum effectively, a 0.5% SA product at pH 3.0 may deliver more active acid to the target than a 2% SA product at pH 4.5.

Most OTC SA products are formulated at pH 3.0–4.0, but the exact pH is rarely disclosed on packaging. This means that concentration comparisons between products are incomplete without pH data — a 4x difference in labeled concentration can be offset or reversed by a 1-unit difference in pH. The marketing focus on concentration percentage, without pH context, is technically accurate but pharmacologically misleading.

What the Evidence Actually Shows

The head-to-head evidence comparing SA concentrations within the OTC range is limited. The available studies suggest that the dose-response curve for comedolytic activity may plateau above 0.5–1%, with diminishing returns at 2%. Critically, none of the comparative studies controlled for formulation pH — meaning the observed differences (or lack thereof) may reflect pH differences rather than concentration effects. The FDA OTC monograph approves the 0.5–2% range without requiring concentration-specific efficacy data.

Tolerability Trade-off

Higher SA concentrations are associated with increased irritation, dryness, and peeling — particularly in sensitive skin types and when combined with other active ingredients (retinoids, AHAs, benzoyl peroxide). The tolerability trade-off is clinically relevant: if 0.5% and 2% produce similar comedolytic outcomes (as the limited evidence suggests), the lower concentration offers a better risk-benefit ratio for most patients. The "maximum strength" marketing framing implies that tolerability is a worthwhile trade-off for superior efficacy — a claim the evidence does not clearly support.

Verdict: Partially Supported

The claim that higher SA concentration delivers better outcomes is partially supported — there is a pharmacological basis for concentration-dependent activity, and some studies show modest superiority of 2% over 0.5% for inflammatory lesions. However, the evidence is limited, pH is a confounding variable that is rarely controlled, and the dose-response curve may plateau within the OTC range. The tolerability trade-off at higher concentrations is not adequately communicated in "maximum strength" marketing. Evidence rating: 3/5.
